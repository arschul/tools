The test at a glance

What is the TOEFL ITP?

The TOEFL ITP (Institutional Testing Program) is a paper-based multiple-choice test of academic English. Every question has four options — A, B, C, D — and you mark your answer by filling in a bubble on an answer sheet. There is no writing and no speaking.

SectionQuestionsTimeWhat it tests
1 · Listening5035 minShort conversations, longer conversations, and mini-talks — heard once only
2 · Structure & Written Expression4025 minGrammar: completing sentences and finding errors
3 · Reading5055 minFive academic passages with comprehension questions

Total: 140 questions in about 2 hours. Scores range from 310 to 677. Each section is scored separately, so improving your weakest section raises your total fastest.

Golden rules for the whole test

  • Never leave a blank. There is no penalty for wrong answers — always guess.
  • Pace yourself. The clock is your biggest opponent, especially in Section 2.
  • Answer in order on the sheet. Skipping a line on the bubble sheet ruins everything after it. Check your question number every few answers.
  • Eliminate first. Crossing out two bad options turns a 25% guess into a 50% guess.

Section 1 · 50 questions · 35 minutes

Listening Comprehension

You hear everything once only — no replays, no transcripts. The audio never stops, so if you miss a question, guess immediately and move on. The section has three parts:

PartFormatQuestions
AShort conversations (two speakers, two lines) + one spoken question30
BLonger conversations (about a minute)~8
CMini-talks and short lectures~12

Part A strategy: the second speaker is the answer

Almost every Part A question asks about the second speaker — what they mean, imply, or will do next. The first speaker sets the scene; the second speaker carries the answer.

Example Woman: Are you coming to the party on Friday?
Man: I wish I could, but I work that night.
Question: What does the man mean? → He can't go to the party.

Notice: the correct answer rephrases the idea. It almost never repeats the exact words you heard. In fact, an option that repeats a word from the audio is often a trap.

Watch out for sound-alike traps

Trap You hear: "I can't bear this cold weather." → A wrong option might mention a beer or a bear (the animal). If an option just sounds like the audio, be suspicious.

Idioms and expressions the ITP loves

Part A is full of everyday idioms. If you know the expression, the question is easy; if not, it's almost impossible. Learn these classics:

Suggestion language = "What will they probably do?"

Patterns Why don't we…? / How about…? / Let's… / We could always… — when you hear these, expect a question about what the speakers will probably do next.

Parts B & C strategy: read ahead

  • While instructions play, skim the next answer options. Options tell you the topic and what to listen for.
  • The first sentence of a talk usually announces the topic — that's the "main purpose" answer.
  • Listen for numbers, names, and reasons; detail questions come in the same order as the audio.
  • Common settings: library, professor's office, registration desk, class lecture, campus tour.

Section 2 · 40 questions · 25 minutes

Structure & Written Expression

This is the grammar section — and the fastest. You have about 35 seconds per question, so pattern recognition beats slow analysis. Two parts:

PartFormatQuestions
StructureA sentence with a blank — choose the option that completes it correctly15
Written ExpressionA sentence with four underlined parts — find the one that is wrong25

Structure questions test whether you can build a correct sentence; Written Expression tests whether you can spot a broken one. The same grammar points power both.

The Big Rule: one subject, one verb

Before anything else, find the main subject and main verb. Most Structure answers simply supply the missing one; most wrong options add an extra one.

CorrectHoney is one of the few foods that never spoils. (subject: Honey, verb: is)
WrongHoney it is one of the few foods… (two subjects — "Honey" and "it")

Point 1 · Subject–verb agreement

The test hides the subject behind a prepositional phrase. Cross the phrase out mentally.

CorrectThe results of the experiment were surprising.
WrongThe results of the experiment was surprising.

Point 2 · Word form: adjective vs. adverb

CorrectShe explained the theory clearly. (adverb describes the verb)
WrongShe explained the theory clear.

Point 3 · Parallel structure

Items joined by and / or / but must have the same form.

CorrectBats use sound to find food, to avoid obstacles, and to communicate.
WrongBats use sound to find food, avoiding obstacles, and to communicate.

Point 4 · Verb tense signals

Signalsin 1492 / ago / last year → simple past · since / for / so far → present perfect · by the time… had → past perfect
WrongColumbus has sailed across the Atlantic in 1492. (a finished date needs simple past: sailed)

Point 5 · Appositives & relative clauses

A noun phrase between commas can rename the subject with no extra verb — or a clause with which / who + verb can describe it.

CorrectThe Sahara, which covers a third of Africa, is the largest hot desert.
Also correctThe Sahara, the largest hot desert on Earth, covers a third of Africa.
WrongThe Sahara, that covers a third of Africa, is… ("that" cannot follow a comma)

Point 6 · Inversion after negative openers

After Not until… / Rarely / Never / Only after… at the start of a sentence, the verb moves before the subject, like a question.

CorrectNot until the 19th century did scientists prove that bacteria cause disease.
WrongNot until the 19th century scientists proved…

Point 7 · Comparatives & the double trap

CorrectThe new computers are much faster than the old ones. · The higher you climb, the thinner the air becomes.
Wrongmuch more faster · more easier · taller from me

Point 8 · Count vs. non-count classics

Never pluralinformation · advice · equipment · furniture · research · knowledge — "informations" is always wrong.

Point 9 · Preposition partners

Memorizedepend on · interested in · capable of · similar to · different from · despite (never "despite of") · because of

Point 10 · Pronoun agreement

CorrectThe committee announced its decision. (a committee = one thing)
WrongEach of the students raised their hand… (on the ITP, "each" is singular: his or her)

How to attack Written Expression

  • Read the whole sentence first — the error is often agreement between two distant words.
  • Check the underlined parts in this order: verbs → word forms → parallelism → pronouns → prepositions.
  • You don't need to correct the error — just find it. Don't waste time rewriting.

Section 3 · 50 questions · 55 minutes

Reading Comprehension

Five academic passages (about 250–350 words each) with roughly ten questions per passage. Budget about 10 minutes per passage — including its questions. The topics are academic (science, history, biography, culture) but every answer is in the text. Never use outside knowledge.

Read the questions like a detective

Each question type has a fixed shape and a fixed strategy:

1 · Main idea

Looks like"What is the main topic of the passage?" / "What is the author's main purpose?"

Strategy: read the first sentence of each paragraph. The correct answer covers the whole passage — options that only match one paragraph are traps.

2 · Detail

Looks like"According to the passage, when did…?" / "The author states that…"

Strategy: pick a keyword from the question (a name, number, or unusual noun), scan for it, and read that sentence carefully. The answer paraphrases the text.

3 · Vocabulary in context

Looks like"The word 'durable' in line 4 is closest in meaning to…"

Strategy: re-read the sentence and replace the word with each option. Context beats dictionary knowledge — a familiar word may be used in an unusual way.

4 · Reference

Looks like"The word 'they' in line 9 refers to…"

Strategy: look backwards for the nearest noun that agrees in number, then test it by substitution.

5 · Inference

Looks like"It can be inferred from the passage that…" / "The author implies…"

Strategy: the answer is one small logical step from the text — never a big leap. If an option needs outside knowledge or says something extreme (always, never, all), it's usually wrong.

6 · Negative fact (NOT / EXCEPT)

Looks like"All of the following are mentioned EXCEPT…"

Strategy: this is a hunting game — three options are in the passage, one is not. Find and eliminate the three. Slow but reliable; save it for last if you're short on time.

Pacing plan

  • Don't read the whole passage deeply first. Skim 30 seconds for topic and layout, then go straight to the questions.
  • Questions follow the order of the passage — question 3's answer is usually after question 2's.
  • If a question eats more than a minute, bubble your best guess and move on. You can circle it in the booklet and return.
